The settlement was formed over 300 years ago, on the site of the final conflict of the Galactic War, a Battle named…The Final Battle. The war itself had been fought over the most valuable of resources, more valuable than fuel, or food, or territory…this war had been fought over sweet ass. It was said that a voracious teen Velitaen princess had seduced a Norn lord and stolen his virginity away before an arranged marriage that would have settled some border disputes. After a century of fighting, the hapless exploring Humans of the Unionised Planets did what Humans did best, they stuck their big fucking noses into other peoples business. They stupidly and well meaningly tried to save a Feldar transport from destruction in the Veil and got themselves introduced to the two biggest powers in the known universe by way of half a century of interstellar war. Then as quickly and as pointlessly as it started…it stopped, and no one but the powers that be knew why.

When the dust settled - both figuratively and literally as a lot of drugs were consumed at the signing of the treaty - Brokepeace was named an independent and neutral territory for all as a sign of collaboration and because dead civilians had been exceedingly bad for profit lines at large corporations. There were two main conditions for peace, the first was the installation of an independent and anonymous judiciary force, picked from the three main galactic powers - named the Pillars of Ilima. The second was a democratically elected leader. Given the 500 years of bloody conflict and all around distrust and hatred, any reasonable person (even Grothgar the Reckless of the Ginger Clan who lost the battle of Hanar when he ordered his entire fleet into a nearby sun when he thought the light was a direct attack on his people) would have believed it nigh impossible. But the three empires did something extremely uncharacteristic - they gave the nomadic people of the Veil a vote. When the Norn, the Velit, and the Union Planets voted for themselves, the Feldar voted for the people that tried to help them during the war, one act of charity, paying dividends.

And so, the Union had controlled Brokepeace for almost 300 years, to the point where the 5-yearly vote seemed as pointless as prayers at JK Rowling’s deathbed. The Union were now voted for by all four parties, mainly due to the proven impartiality and success of Union rule, the Velit and Norn not wanting the other to take control, and the fact that the Union had made it a capital offence to not say thank you when someone holds a door open for you. The current ruler of Brokepeace was none other than Kellan’s own father, Korvis Amray.

As Kellan watched the planet of Ilima grow large through the viewscreen of Wrath’s ship, The Gloombreaker, she was simultaneously hit with a deep joy at the sight of the crystal buildings and blue oceans, and intense anxiety of having to face her family again.

Wrath caught her clutching her stomach uncomfortably. “If you are going to vomit, I suggest you do it in the airlock. That way, I can space the mess and the offender at the same time.”

“It’s just felt more real since we left Agent Princess back on Velit.” Kellan shewed on her fingernail. “You think they’ll treat me any differently?”

“Considering you are still a burdensome trouble maker with all the use of lipstick on a horses’ arsehole, I think not.” Wrath stuck the ship on autopilot and spun her chair with a tired sigh. “I once returned home after a…difficult ordeal. Everything felt wrong, the passage of time felt more keenly by me than others due to my age. On reflection, given the circumstances, my family tried their best. But parents are not infallible, regardless of what ideals we have. They are idiots, just the same as we are.”

Kellan sat. “I guess that makes me feel a little better.”

Wrath grunted. “It wasn’t meant to. You are going to have a terrible, awful day. But at the end of it, you will be stronger to face the next thing.”

“You’re an inspiration, Wrath.” Kellan rolled her eyes, and left the cockpit to grab her bags.

Wrath started after her. “Gods, I hope not, kid.” She whispered.

General Etti Amray looked at the plate of Velitean cream puffs her husband had brought her and wondered if a third would make her burst out of her breastplate. Why had the gods given her enough willpower to command an interstellar army but not to resist those sugary demons? Korvis Amray, the Duke of Brokepeace, her husband and best friend, stroked his immaculate beard and then plonked himself down into the chair at the farside of her office. He was restless, and he crossed and uncrossed his legs. “I got word from the guard - she’s back. What time do you think she’ll be here?”

Etti smiled wryly, trying not to look Korvis in the eyes - all their conversations about Kellan ended in argument lately. “I asked Wrath to bring her right to us, but you and I know Wrath only does what she thinks is best for her.”

Korvis exhaled a laugh, then gave Etti a stare. “Well we can’t blame Wrath for being self motivated, given the circumstances. She has a large stake in this.” Korvis’ face softened. “I’m glad Kellan is back though, aren’t you?”

“No. She should have stayed away.”

Korvis stood. He was clearly annoyed, but not at her, it was the situation. “I’m sure she’ll love to hear you say that.”

Etti hated this - this talking. She wasn’t one for sharing feelings and having conversations and all that trash. Put a rifle and a claymore in her hands and she would convey her feelings accurately enough - when you maimed an enemy they knew exactly how you felt. There were days when conflict was far easier than family. “You know what I meant, Kovis. We should send her back to the station and leave her there until we can sort all this.”

“Nice to know things never change. Parents are still dicks and make decisions about their kids when they’re not in the room.” Kellan pushed the door to Etti’s office open. It had been ajar, how long had she been there? Kellan nodded to each of them in turn. “Dad. General.”

Korvis went to his daughter and swept her up in his arms, then grabbed her face to look at her, assessing each feature in turn. He was such a good father, it made Etti’s eye’s momentarily water until she blinked her composure back.

“You have no idea how glad I am to see you. I’ve missed you so much.” Korvis said.

“Glad one of you has.” Gods, Kellan looked at her with such unbridled hatred. But her daughter had become so beautiful - the awkward, lofty teen she had been now a lithe and striking young woman. Etti wanted to go to her, but she stood firm. There was a time and a place, and this wasn’t it.

Etti breathed a deep sigh, they were so similar, so eager to fight, to give a killing blow with each sentence. Though Kellan wasn’t born to the family, and though parents weren’t meant to have a favourite but absolutely did, Etti wondered if she loved Kellan the most out of all of them.

“Kellan, you have some…matters to discuss with your mother, but I’ll be waiting outside for a quick chat.” Korvis went to grab his overcoat and walked as he talked, never a fan of doing one job when two could be done. “I have business in the senate with the Feldar, they’re coming around to the idea of joining the Union.”

Etti rounded her desk, suddenly overcome. She held her husband, too much to say in too little time. “Korvis, do…do you have to? Can’t you send another in your place? Today of all days.”

Korvis smiled, then kissed his beloved on the forehead. “Etti. You know I have to go, there's too much at stake if I don’t. You’ll both be fine.” He looked over to Kellan and gave her a wink, then turned and left, closing the giant doors to Etti’s office behind him.

Etti took a few seconds to compose herself, knowing this was going to be as much fun as experimental butt surgery. She took a deep, settling breath and gestured to the chair, but Kellan stood there with her arms crossed.

“So you didn’t want me to come home, huh? What’s the matter, am I a tarnish on one of those medals you pin to your chestplate? Perhaps they can give you a new one for the most inconsistent mother? Or maybe it’s just easier to shove the kids you didn’t birth under the rug?”

Etti put her head in her hands, it was just the same as when they’d sent her to the watch station. Kellan’s pain was so raw, so exposed. This wasn’t how she wanted things to go. “You were sent out there for your own good, Kellan.”

Kellan picked up a cream cake and shoved it in her mouth in one. “Yeah, it worked wonders. I worked on my novel, got some sick abs, started eating right. And here I was thinking that you sent me out there because you blamed me for Ashr’s death.”

Kellan pushed her mother’s hand away. “You know what they say - ‘whatever doesn’t kill you either makes you immune-compromised or completely distrustful of every being you once loved’.”

Etti forced her daughter into the chair, then perched on the end of her desk, she blew out air and waited until Kellan was looking at her. “Ashr’s death wasn’t your fault.”

“Don’t ‘Good Will Hunting’ me, mother.” Kellan laughed a sick laugh. “You’re not as lovable as Robin Williams.”

“So you sent me to the border so you didn’t have to look at me.”

“No, we did it to save your life!” Etti had said too much, a hand rushing to her mouth. Kellan looked afraid, Etti unable to let things stay like this. She put a hand on her daughters. “Kellan, your life was in danger because of what happened.”

“Because of an accident?!” Kellan threw her head back and laughed hard. “You’ve fed me some shit before, but this is the dung souffle of lies. Where has this brown nugget come from then?”

Etti had come too far, she had to tell her now. “Wrath told us.”

Kellan stood, her face twisted by hate and betrayal. Much as she’d hate to admit it, Wrath was probably one of the only people Kellan could call friend, to feel so betrayed by her would be like salt in the wound. She resolved, right there, to tell The Pillars that she had taken her to see the Rift in The Veil. She would have revenge, petty or otherwise, for her lie of omission for this year.

Etti reached for her, but she pulled away. “We’ll just see about that.”
