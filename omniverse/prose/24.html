Tamia jumped onto the bonnet of a passing transport, then propelled herself up in the air to wall run off the double decked community vessel. She used the scaling claw Ludwig had made her weeks ago to dig and hold onto the side as it slowly crawled down the busy public highway. The advert next to her showed a reclining woman in a swimsuit and sunglasses holding a meal replacement drink.

“This is intolerable.” Hijoki declared, shouting against the mid morning rain, it always rained here. “How do these people stand for being so insulted all the time? I’m always bikini body ready!”

“You’re a slightly greying sock, you have no place on the beach.” Tamia smiled.

“Where the hell would you store your money and keys?” Hijoki looked aghast as Tamia vaulted to land atop a passing medical transport. There it was ahead of them, their target.

“Well, Lionel naturally hates the sea, sun and sand, so he could watch it while I swim.” At Hijoki’s hurt grumble, she kissed the old sock atop his head. “Of course we’d take you, you grumpy old foot-fetishist.”

Their quarry turned off the main concourse and stopped, so Tamia jumped to the light pole, swinging around it before leaping into the air and landing a perfect dismount. As the courier stepped down from his vessel, Tami grabbed him by his shirt and pinned him to the side of the transport. “You inept fool. I have been waiting for that delivery all morning, and you didn’t even knock at the door. Where is your honour?”

The courier was trembling. “I…I pushed the bell, darling.”

“You mean the bell that says ‘out of order, please knock loudly’?” She eyed the side of his conveyance. “UPS. Does that stand for Unorganised Pointless Shitbirds?”

“Tamia!”

She turned to the voice, to find Lionel in a suit and tie. He was staring at her, incredulous, along with everyone else walking down from London Bridge Station.

Mookie lay breathless on their front atop the plateau, looking out into the nebula of this reality they called the Edge of Forever. It was a beautiful pinkish-green cloud-like nebula that vaguely looked like groups of dogs doing it in their natural style. It was an uncanny resemblance, and Mookie felt second hand embarrassed looking at it. Doots picked up her Keytar and started plucking at the strings, changing the nebula’s colours to vivid reds. “Me wanting you isn’t anything new. You were, are, and always will be beautiful to me, Mooks. You being comfortable is the most important thing.”

Mookie sighed.

“What is it?” Doots asked.

A shadow fell over them. “Don’t mind me, I am just catching sunbeams.” Stoker plonked down next to them, rolling out a beach towel and slathering sunscreen over his hairy insect body.

“Stoker!” Mookie said as they grabbed their robe, Doots using her keytar to hide her modesty.

When you’re billions of eons old and madder than a box of boomer conservatives angry at hypothetical situations of their own making, you have sexual drive of taxidermy snail. On a long enough timeline, we’re all asexual, believe Stoker.”

Doots kicked angry at his sunscreen bottle, which left the gravity of their asteroid and floated into space. “We wanted some quiet time.”

“No, you wanted the sexing time. I calculated you’d need no longer than 20 minutes as is optimal for the romantic sex. Was I wrong?” Atlas shrugged, looking over his sunglasses.

Doots and Mookie looked at each other, feeling abashed by his accurate but wholly inappropriate assessment of their sex life.

Mookie went to talk, but Stoker put his foreleg up. “Before am turning invisible, do you have beer for Stoker in that cooler…”

“No.” They both said, hoping their expression would warn off any further interruptions.

Stoker waved at them dismissively. “So rude to poor Stoker.”

Doots shook her head and looked over at Mooks, who was pulling their robe back on. “I feel bad. That we’re enjoying ourselves while the rest are…”

Fighting the literal embodiment of all despair and loss in the multiverse was taking its toll, we just lost Ellie. We had to strike a…balance.” Doots grabbed Mookie’s confused face in her hands and squeezed. “Oh, Mooks, that’s it!”

Stoker sat up and pulled his sunglasses down. “You find beer after all?”

The thought - his previously unshakable code - felt foreign in his brain, like a tumour or when religious people just follow a code written to honour their imaginary friend. “Let’s just finish this.” He muttered.

“Steelsquid 2, this is Steelsquid 1.” Ludwig spoke over the pocket radio as he slithered through the air duct before dropping down into the hermetically sealed lab.

“I was of the understanding that I was Steelsquid 1.” Heath was in the lab on board the Swiftfeather, standing in front of the monolith of technology the pair of them had created, wrestling with the burned out semiconductor that they needed to replace.

“I told you that I was going to be number 1 this time.” Ludwig’s voice squawked back through the radio. “Plus, 1 is the loneliest number. I’m doing you a favour.”

“I have it on good authority that 2 can be as bad as 1.”

Ludwig swore under his breath. Damn Lionel leaving his spotify account unlocked for them, it had been a bone of contention for the pair. When they had all agreed to take a break from worrying about the Technomancer, Ludwig and Heath had chosen to stay in a reality where time moved slower and there were no lifeforms left, so as to complete their experiments. But six months together without their friends under such pressure had been difficult. But this day in particular was more stressful, they only had one day left to swipe the semiconductor, install it, do what they needed to do, and finish the last two episodes of Downtown Abbey.

The lab was stifling with the typical white sterility of a lab, but the entire floor spreading out in front of him was coated in a thick layer of what Ludwig hoped was dust. “Heath, the lights if you please?”

Heath quickly tapped into the facility’s systems and turned every halogen bulb in the place on, each one flickering on with a slow hum. And as they came on, Ludwig wished they hadn’t. At the end of the corridor, a gigantic monolith of hardware stood proud, with a long broadsword buried into it. The wielder of the sword still grasped the hilt in clasped hands, but had died hundreds of years ago, his skeleton wrapped in a blue half cape guilded with what was once golden thread. He wasn’t the only corpse in the room. Five metres in front of Ludwig a skeletal humanoid bull was slouched over a bank of computers, arrows sticking from his ribcage. Next to him, lay a small cherubic angel, wings still perfectly intact as if rot could never touch them. He reached for another skeleton, who clutched at his stomach, a long rifle draped over them.. Ludwig surveyed the rest of the room, gasping as he noticed a handle half buried in a pile of deactivated robotic soldiers. As he pulled it out of the rubble, he knew what it was instantly - the pistol of a Duke of Lead, or their equivalent. This was the last stand of this reality’s versions of them against their Technomancer. The scene didn’t exactly give Ludwig confidence, this reality was devoid of all life - had this battle caused that?

Ludwig reached for the semiconductor from the giant tower of tech their Technomancer had been, trepidatiously pulling it from the base. “Heath, I’m coming home.” He whispered into his communicator as he walked to the air duct, not wanting to disturb this graveyard any more than he had, and wondering if this was a sign of things to come for them.

“Tam, you can’t just assault people who annoy you.” Lionel sighed as he put his work bag down on the floor of the coffee shop. “This isn’t Ithia or Brackenfield Station, or a Walmart carpark. We’ve been here for eight months now, you don’t need to fight against everything.”

Tamia slammed her katana down on the cafe table. “I need to do something, Lionel. I have tried to fit in on your alternate earth, but this planet is not for me. The people are obnoxious and stupid, believing that they are the only life in the universe.”

“Some of them defy all reason.” Hijoki said. “Dogs in baby carriages, people who think gender is defined by their birth biology, recreational golf. It’s craziness! Fighting the Technomancer made more sense.”

Lionel turned away. “Don’t say that. We’re done, we came here to be free of that, the fighting, the guilt.”

It was obvious what he meant, but Tamia was staying quiet. She knew how difficult this was for Lionel, he had been blaming himself for Ellie’s death a long time now. He wondered if by breaking up, had Ellie lost her last reason to stay with the crew and decided it was better to sacrifice herself? Either way, it had been Ellie’s decision, and he shouldn’t have felt bad. But grief had a way of churning up your insides faster than a caviar and cheese milkshake.

“Maybe you should try work again?” Lionel suggested, even though his heart wasn’t in the statement.

“I tried work.” Tamia muttered.

“You were an emergency services responder and you kept encouraging people to take up arms against their assaulters, you prompted a two month crimewave in London and massive police reforms.” Lionel muttered.

“Good! The police should reform, from being facists to being dead fascists.”

“Nice one kid.” Hijoki made himself into a fist and Tamia fist bumped his head. “Thanks.”

Lionel scratched his head. Tamia had always cut his hair back on the Swiftfeather, but now he was back on a version of Earth, he had his hair cut by a barber. Lionel’s shorn bonce now looked like someone had hacked at it with a Harvester’s dismembered talons, and he rubbed at it whenever he was uncomfortable. “Ignore me, I’m just tetchy about this haircut. He cut it too short, it’s like looking into the future.”

“I don’t know how you don’t kill half those assholes. Even Grissom Graves would not ask you to clean a stained toilet bowl after he used it. Why are we here, Lionel?”

“Because I can’t lose you too.” He shouted, then cleared his throat. “Either of you.”

“Thanks, kid. Was kinda feeling like a third wheel here.” Hijoki said.

Tamia went to him and threw her arms around Lionel, burying his face into a chest so boobdacious that few had been lucky enough to get this close to it without regretting it and lamenting their lost fingers later. “You can’t protect us. We want to fight, we don’t want to sit in this reality, despite how comfortable life is here, despite all President Reynolds says about protecting citizens.”

“President who?” Lionel narrowed his eyes.

Tami threw him today’s newspaper. On the front page the British Prime Minister was shaking hands with Ryan Reynolds in the White House. He smiled that stupid smile he had, as if his entire being was vacant for the winter while his brain was vacationing somewhere sunnier. Lionel slammed the table and went into his room to grab a bag. “Let’s go. Fucking worse than the Technomancer here.”

He pressed a button on a metal device which opened a portal in front of them.

The Technomancer sat on the beach of what was - before he had razed it to the ground in a temper tantrum - the Dwarven resort town of Snarfholt, counting the volcanic black rocks. It was the only way he could keep sane, well, that and conversing with the weird talking dog that seemed to appear when the sun was at the highest point in the sky. Sometimes he was a dog, sometimes an indistinguishable black blob in his vision caused by lack of food, water, and sleep. He picked up the 67,433,632,785…no wait, 786th rock…then skimmed it into the water. He sighed a deep, teenage sigh and flumped down to the ground.

“What’s up, Technomancer?” a voice said at his side. The dog, who had for some reason today wore a wide brimmed hat, wagged his tail and then broke off a piece of the hat, dipped it in the top, which was filled with melted cheese, and ate it.

“I don’t know, Malcolm, I really thought they’d be here by now. It’s like I meant nothing to them. What has a nemesis got to do to be relevant nowadays? ”

The Dog grew another leg and wrapped it around the Technomancer’s shoulders. “I’m sure they’ll be here soon, who could forget a hideous tyrant like you?” He grabbed the Technomancers chrome skull and shook it. “Now who’s my precious boy? Who is it?” The dog said in a singsong voice.

The Technomancer looked at the ground, bashfully. “Is…is it me?”

“The Dog pulled the Technomancer to his feet. “You’re damn straight it is, soldier. Now you pick yourself up, dust yourself off, and be what you were meant to be…a goddamned fabulous and genocidal son of a bitch.”

Suddenly, above them, a golden window appeared in the sky in a flash. When it was gone, and when the light faded, the Swiftfeather hung in the atmosphere above. The Technomancer smiled, and clicked his taloned fingers. In response, hundreds of dead climbed out of the rocks around him.
